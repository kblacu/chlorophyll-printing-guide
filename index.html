Chlorophyll
Printing
Guide

By Kimberly Blacutt

A chemical-free photographic process where images are developed on fresh leaves using sunlight and the natural photosensitivity of chlorophyll.

Assembly

The assembly follows a specific sequence: a flat base board, then a fresh leaf, a transparency, and a clear panel that flattens the transparency against the leaf while allowing sunlight through. Finally, clamps secure the stack with even pressure while the print develops.

Clamps ▼

Maintain consistent, even pressure and alignment throughout the exposure period.

Binder clips work well for this.

Clear Panel ▼

Presses the transparency against the leaf while allowing UV light to pass through for exposure.

Use glass or clear acrylic. Must be transparent to UV light.

Transparency ▼

This image acts as a light mask, the dark areas of the image protect areas beneath it from UV exposure.

Clear acetate prints are available at most print shops. High contrast images have best results.

Leaves ▼

Fresh, flat specimens with smooth surfaces work best for clear image transfer.

Recommended: maple, philodendron, hostas. Avoid fuzzy or waxy leaves.

Base Board ▼

Provides stable backing and even pressure distribution across the entire assembly.

Use moisture wicking materials like wood.

Exposure

Sunlight ▼

The photosensitivity of chlorophyll is what makes this printing process possible. Strong sunlight breaks down the molecules, creating the image.

Direct sunlight is most effective. You can prop your assembled stack in front of a window or place it outside in the sunlight.

Time ▼

Development time varies greatly depending on environmental conditions, but the typical range is between 2-7 days.

Higher sun intensity develops the images faster. Cloudy days may disrupt or slower the process. Thicker leaves take longer to develop.
